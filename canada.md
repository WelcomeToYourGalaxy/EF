# HOW COMMUNITIES CAN STOP DESTRUCTIVE DEVELOPMENT PROJECTS IN CANADA

**Complete Community Opposition How-To**

This is a national guide. Canada splits authority between the federal government and thirteen provinces and territories, so almost every fight happens on two or three tracks at once — federal, provincial, and municipal. The good news is that this same layering gives communities *more* points of leverage, not fewer, and Canada has one legal tool that is genuinely among the strongest in the world: the constitutional rights of Indigenous peoples. Projects that clear every economic and political hurdle still die in Canada when consultation is done badly. That is the thread running through everything below.

Nothing here is legal advice. It is a map of how the system works and where ordinary people have made it bend.

---

## The lay of the land

**What gets fought.** Pipelines and oil-and-gas infrastructure, mines (including the surge in "critical minerals" projects), LNG terminals and export facilities, large dams and transmission lines, highways and port expansions, logging and old-growth cutblocks, landfills and incinerators, quarries and aggregate pits, and — increasingly — data centres and warehouse sprawl on farmland and wetlands.

**Who decides.** A single project can touch all three levels of government:

- **Federal** — through the **Impact Assessment Agency of Canada (IAAC)** for "designated projects" with effects in federal jurisdiction, and the **Canada Energy Regulator (CER)** for interprovincial and international pipelines and power lines. Nuclear projects run through the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission.
- **Provincial** — each province runs its own environmental-assessment (EA) regime and issues most of the permits that actually let dirt move: water licences, mine permits, forestry cutting permits, air and waste approvals.
- **Municipal** — zoning, rezoning, official-plan amendments, development permits, and local by-laws. This is where the most winnable early fights live, because councils are small, local, and up for re-election.

**The honest picture.** Most projects that get proposed do eventually proceed in some form. But "as proposed" is the exception, not the rule — sustained opposition routinely forces smaller footprints, binding conditions, monitoring, buffer zones, financial assurances, and outright withdrawals. And Canada has a real graveyard of "inevitable" megaprojects: the Northern Gateway pipeline, the $15.7-billion Energy East pipeline, and the Teck Frontier oilsands mine were all abandoned after opposition made them politically and legally untenable. The Trans Mountain expansion was *quashed by a federal court* mid-stream. None of that happened by accident, and none of it happened quickly. Plan for a marathon.

## Step 1 — Map the project and find who actually decides

You cannot fight what you cannot see. Before anything else, pin down exactly what is being proposed, who has to approve it, and where in the process it currently sits.

**Find the file.**

- **Federal impact assessments** are public on the **Canadian Impact Assessment Registry (CIAR)**, run by IAAC (start at the agency's site, *canada.ca/en/impact-assessment-agency*). Every designated project has a registry page with the proponent's documents, the agency's notices, and — crucially — the public **comment periods**.
- **Pipelines and power lines** are on the **Canada Energy Regulator**'s public record (*cer-rec.gc.ca*), including every application and every letter filed against it.
- **Provincial projects** appear on that province's EA registry or environmental registry (for example, Ontario posts proposals on the Environmental Registry of Ontario; British Columbia runs assessments through its Environmental Assessment Office; Quebec routes major projects to the **BAPE**, the *Bureau d'audiences publiques sur l'environnement*). Search "[your province] environmental assessment registry" and "[your project name]."
- **Municipal projects** are in council agendas, planning-committee minutes, and development-application trackers on the municipality's website.

**Identify the real decision-makers.** For each approval, learn: which agency or council holds the pen, what the legal test is (federal impact assessments now turn on *adverse effects within federal jurisdiction* after the 2024 amendments — see Step 4), what the deadline for public input is, and who the individual officials and elected representatives are. Names, not just offices.

**Check for the pressure points.**

- **Money and ownership.** Who is the proponent? Who finances and insures them? Publicly traded companies answer to shareholders; insurers and banks can be pressured to walk away (this is exactly how campaigners chipped at Trans Mountain).
- **Timelines.** Every project has permit-expiry dates, financing deadlines, and seasonal construction windows. Delay is a weapon: projects that stall lose investors.
- **Conflicts and irregularities.** Undisclosed meetings between proponents and regulators, board members with industry ties, or consultation that exists on paper only. Revealing exactly this — private meetings between a pipeline company and National Energy Board members — is what collapsed Energy East.

**Log everything** in a shared timeline from day one: every filing, notice, comment deadline, and public statement. Freedom-of-information requests (federal *Access to Information*; each province has its own FOI law) can pry loose correspondence, staff reports, and modelling the proponent would rather keep quiet.

## Step 2 — Document the impacts

Assessments are won and lost on evidence. The proponent will file a glossy impact statement; your job is to find what it left out and to build an independent record.

**Establish the baseline before construction.** Photograph and date the site and waterways as they are now. Record what lives there and who uses it — species, wetlands, drinking-water sources, farmland, trails, fishing and hunting grounds. Baselines are the single most valuable thing a community can create early, because they cannot be reconstructed later.

**Attack the impact statement on its own terms.** Read the proponent's documents closely and look for: underestimated water use or contamination pathways, cumulative effects the study ignores (this project *plus* everything else already in the watershed), downplayed effects on species at risk, traffic and dust and noise, and climate emissions. Cumulative effects and effects on species at risk are recurring weak points in Canadian filings.

**Bring in independent expertise.** Hydrogeologists, biologists, air-quality specialists, and health researchers can produce reports that carry weight in a hearing. Universities, retired professionals, and the science staff at the legal organizations below are all reachable. A single credible independent report can force an agency to send a proponent back for more study.

**Document the human and economic case.** Effects on health, on property values, on existing local industries (tourism, fishing, farming, recreation), and on community wellbeing all belong on the record. So does the counter-economics: many projects promise jobs that are mostly temporary and mostly filled from outside.

**Indigenous knowledge and land use.** Where a project affects First Nations, Métis, or Inuit territory, traditional-use and traditional-knowledge studies are not a nice-to-have — they are central to the legal duty to consult (Step 4) and often to the strongest expert evidence about what a place actually contains.

## Step 3 — Build the coalition

A lone objector is easy to dismiss. A broad, disciplined coalition is not. Canadian fights are won by the range of people standing together as much as by the arguments.

**Start local and concrete.** Neighbours, a ratepayers' or residents' association, local farmers, anglers and hunters, cottagers, small businesses that depend on the landscape. Give people a specific, winnable early ask — show up to a council meeting, sign onto a comment letter, host a lawn sign.

**Build the alliances that change the math.**

- **Indigenous nations and organizations.** Where a project crosses or affects Indigenous territory, the affected nation is not a stakeholder to be recruited — it is a rights-holder with constitutional standing, and its leadership decides its own path. Non-Indigenous groups support that leadership when invited; they do not speak for it. This relationship, done with respect and on the nation's terms, has been decisive in the biggest Canadian wins.
- **Environmental organizations** with legal and scientific muscle (see the list at the end) — Ecojustice, West Coast Environmental Law, the Canadian Environmental Law Association, and regional groups.
- **Health voices** — the Canadian Association of Physicians for the Environment and local doctors and nurses.
- **Labour, faith communities, and municipal politicians** who can lend legitimacy and reach.

**Organize to last.** Pick a clear name and a simple, accurate message. Divide the work — research, legal, media, fundraising, turnout. Keep decisions transparent so the coalition does not fracture. Fundraise early: hearings, experts, and lawyers cost money, and small local fundraising also builds the base. Assume the campaign will run for years and build something that can survive burnout and turnover.

**Stay strictly non-violent and lawful in framing.** In Canada, the credibility of the *process* case — "we were not properly consulted, the science is incomplete" — is the campaign's biggest asset. Don't hand the proponent a reason to change the subject.

## Step 4 — Legal and procedural challenges

This is where Canada is distinctive, and where communities have real teeth.

### Use the participation rights you're given

Both federal regulators build public participation into the process, and using it well creates the record you'll need later.

- **Impact assessments (IAAC).** The agency runs a multi-stage process with formal **public comment periods** and encourages participation; it also acts as the **Crown Consultation Coordinator** for Indigenous consultation. Comments filed on the record shape the conditions — and become evidence if the decision is later challenged.
- **Canada Energy Regulator hearings.** Members of the public can take part as a **Commentator** (file a *letter of comment* setting out your concerns) or, for those more directly affected, as an **Intervenor** — a role that carries the right to be heard, to receive the decision and its reasons, and in some cases to have participation costs funded. Intervenor status is powerful: it lets you test the proponent's evidence directly.

Filing well — specific, evidence-based, on time — matters more than filing loudly.

### The duty to consult: Canada's strongest lever

Section 35 of the *Constitution Act, 1982* recognizes and affirms the existing Aboriginal and treaty rights of Indigenous peoples. From it flows the **Crown's duty to consult and accommodate** Indigenous peoples whenever a decision might adversely affect their rights (the doctrine set out in *Haida Nation v. British Columbia*). This duty is **constitutional** — legislation cannot abrogate it, and even Canada's recent fast-track laws for "national interest" projects expressly leave it in place.

What this means in practice:

- When a project affects Indigenous rights and the Crown consults inadequately, courts can and do **quash the approval**. The landmark case is the **Trans Mountain expansion**: in 2018 the **Federal Court of Appeal** (*Tsleil-Waututh Nation v. Canada*) overturned the federal approval because Canada had failed to meaningfully consult First Nations and because the regulator had wrongly excluded marine-shipping impacts. The project only proceeded after the government redid the consultation.
- **Consultation is not a veto.** Canadian law does not (yet) require Indigenous *consent* for projects on most Crown land — the duty is to consult and, where appropriate, accommodate. The distinction matters: campaigns that overstate it can be blindsided.
- **UNDRIP is changing the ground underneath this.** The federal *United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act* (2021) and British Columbia's *Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act* (DRIPA, 2019) commit governments to align their laws with UNDRIP and its principle of **free, prior and informed consent**. Courts are still working out how much independent legal force these have — the *Gitxaala* litigation over B.C.'s mineral-claims system, and the political fights over DRIPA in 2025–26, show this is a live, fast-moving area. Get current advice before relying on it.

### Judicial review

Most legal challenges to Canadian project approvals are **applications for judicial review** — asking a court to find the decision unlawful because the decision-maker exceeded its authority, ignored required factors, breached procedural fairness, or (for Indigenous applicants) failed the duty to consult. Federal decisions go to the **Federal Court** or **Federal Court of Appeal**; provincial decisions go to that province's superior court.

- **Standing.** Canadian courts recognize **public-interest standing**, so a community group or NGO — not only a directly affected landowner — can often bring a case where a serious justiciable issue is raised and there's no better-placed challenger.
- **The narrowed federal test.** After the Supreme Court's 2023 *Reference re Impact Assessment Act* found parts of the IAA unconstitutional, the 2024 amendments (in force **June 20, 2024**) refocused federal assessments on **adverse effects within federal jurisdiction** and removed cross-border greenhouse-gas effects from that definition. Know which effects are now federally reviewable and which have shifted to the province — it changes where you fight.
- **Get lawyers early.** Deadlines for judicial review are short (often measured in weeks from the decision), and the organizations below take strong cases **free of charge**.

### Provincial and municipal tracks

Don't neglect them. A federal approval is worthless to a proponent who can't get a provincial water licence or a municipal rezoning. Councils can be moved by turnout and by the next election; provincial permits each have their own appeal and comment routes.

## Step 5 — Media and public pressure

Regulators and politicians respond to what the public sees. In Canada, a strong independent-media ecosystem covers exactly these fights.

**Know the outlets that cover this beat.**

- **The Narwhal** (*thenarwhal.ca*) — an independent, ad-free non-profit newsroom devoted to environmental and natural-world investigative journalism, with bureaus across the country. It exists to hold this kind of decision-making to account.
- **Canada's National Observer** (*nationalobserver.com*) — independent investigative outlet whose reporting on secret proponent–regulator meetings helped collapse the Energy East pipeline.
- **CBC** and regional and Indigenous-run outlets (such as APTN) for reach into general and community audiences.

**Make the story easy to tell.** Reporters need a clear conflict, credible documents, real people affected, and good visuals. Package your baseline photos, your independent expert reports, and one or two spokespeople who can speak plainly. A leaked document or an FOI revelation is worth a hundred press releases.

**Own your own channels too.** A simple website and a mailing list keep supporters informed and mobilizable for comment deadlines and hearing dates. Op-eds and letters to the editor in local papers reach the councillors and MLAs who actually decide.

**Escalate deliberately.** Public comment → letters and op-eds → a packed council or hearing room → rallies → coordinated pressure on financiers and insurers. Each step raises the political and financial cost of the project without ever ceding the moral and procedural high ground.

## Key organizations and resources

**Free legal help (they represent communities at no charge):**

- **Ecojustice** — *ecojustice.ca* — Canada's largest environmental-law charity; lawyers and scientists who litigate at every level of court, free of charge. National.
- **West Coast Environmental Law (WCEL)** — *wcel.org* — free legal advice, plus grants through its **Environmental Dispute Resolution Fund** to help communities pay for their own lawyers; works at the intersection of Indigenous and Canadian law.
- **Canadian Environmental Law Association (CELA)** — *cela.ca* — free legal services for lower-income and vulnerable communities in Ontario, funded by Legal Aid Ontario; founded 1970.
- **East Coast Environmental Law** — Atlantic Canada.

**Health:** Canadian Association of Physicians for the Environment (**CAPE**).

**Case law and legal research:** **CanLII** — *canlii.org* — free access to Canadian court and tribunal decisions.

**The registries (find and track the file):**

- Impact Assessment Agency of Canada / Canadian Impact Assessment Registry — *canada.ca/en/impact-assessment-agency*
- Canada Energy Regulator — *cer-rec.gc.ca*
- Your province's environmental-assessment or environmental registry, and your municipality's planning portal.

**Access to records:** federal *Access to Information* requests, and each province's Freedom of Information law.

## What actually works — an honest assessment

- **Early beats late.** The cheapest project to stop is the one that hasn't yet locked in financing, permits, and political capital. Get organized before the impact statement is filed, not after the approval.
- **Process is the pressure point.** Canadian projects rarely fall on "this is a bad idea." They fall on *incomplete consultation, ignored cumulative effects, flawed process* — the things courts and regulators can act on. Build that record relentlessly.
- **Indigenous rights are the sharpest tool in the box** — and they belong to Indigenous nations, who lead their own fights. Respectful alliance, not appropriation, is both the right posture and the effective one.
- **Delay is a form of victory.** Every month of stall bleeds investor confidence. Several "inevitable" Canadian megaprojects died not from a single knockout but from accumulated cost, risk, and political exhaustion.
- **You will not usually get a clean "no."** More often you win a smaller footprint, hard conditions, buffers, monitoring, funded oversight, or a proponent who quietly walks away. Those are real wins. Count them.

Stopping a project is hard, and the odds favour the proponent on any single day. But the record of the last decade is unambiguous: in Canada, determined communities — especially in alliance with the nations whose rights the Constitution protects — have repeatedly stopped things that were supposed to be unstoppable.
